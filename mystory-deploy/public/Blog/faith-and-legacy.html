Faith & Legacy

What Faith Has to Do With Leaving a Legacy

By MyStory.Family · April 2025 · 5 min read

Hebrews 11 is sometimes called the Hall of Faith.

It's a remarkable passage — a procession of men and women who lived and died trusting in promises they never saw fulfilled. Abel, Noah, Abraham, Moses. People who gave everything to something they could not yet see.

And then the chapter ends with a sentence that should give all of us pause:

"These were all commended for their faith, yet none of them received what had been promised, since God had planned something better for us so that only together with us would they be made perfect."

Together with us. The story was not complete without the next generation. Which means someone had to carry it.

Legacy is not optional for people of faith

We tend to think of leaving a legacy as a nice thing to do — a generous impulse, a kindness to our family. But for a person of faith, it is something closer to a calling.

What you have believed, endured, prayed through, and held onto is not just your personal story. It is part of a larger story that God has been telling across generations. And the next chapter of that story depends, in part, on whether someone carries what you know.

The promises God gave you. The moments of miraculous provision. The prayers he answered in ways you didn't expect. The faith that held when circumstances didn't.

If that goes unwritten, it doesn't just disappear from your family's history. It disappears from your family's faith.

The generation that doesn't know the promises

There is a danger in every generation — the danger of inheriting a faith without understanding its foundations. Of believing because your parents believed, without knowing what they actually believed or why.

The book of Judges uses a sobering phrase to describe what happens when legacy is broken: "Another generation grew up who knew neither the Lord nor what he had done."

They didn't lose their faith through rebellion. They lost it through silence. Nobody told them.

Your life story is an act of prevention. It is the antidote to a generation that grows up not knowing what the promises were.

What a faith legacy actually looks like

A legacy of faith doesn't require a dramatic testimony. It doesn't require a life free of doubt or failure. In fact, the most powerful faith stories often include both.

It looks like telling your grandchildren about the year the money ran out and somehow the bills got paid. About the prayer you prayed for twenty years before you saw the answer. About the moment you almost walked away from faith — and what brought you back.

It looks like naming the specific things you have trusted God for, so the next generation knows what to look for as those promises unfold in their own lives.

It looks like honest testimony: this is what I believed. This is what I endured. This is what I saw God do. This is what I am still trusting him for.

You are a witness

The word "witness" in the New Testament doesn't just mean someone who tells others about their faith. It comes from the Greek word martys — the same root as martyr. A witness is someone who testifies to what they have personally seen and experienced, regardless of the cost.

You are a witness. You have seen things. You have lived things. You have walked through seasons that would have broken someone who had nothing to hold onto — and you held on.

That testimony belongs to your family. It belongs to the generations who will come after you and need to know that faithfulness is possible, that God keeps his promises, that the story continues.

Write it down. Tell it. Don't let it disappear.
